### Incentives Matter
People respond predictably to incentives. If a price signal encourages a certain behavior (e.g., increased production), you can expect to see more of that behavior. Conversely, disincentives will reduce activity. Consider how government programs or futures market positions create incentives.

### Interactions are Key
Your marketing decisions don't happen in a vacuum. They interact with the decisions of other farmers, processors, and end-users. Understanding these interactions is vital for predicting price movements and optimizing your strategy.

### Law of One Price
In a perfectly competitive market, identical goods should trade at the same price in all locations, after accounting for transportation costs. Price differences create arbitrage opportunities.

### Indifference Principle
Consumers are indifferent between sources of a commodity if the price is the same, regardless of origin. This drives price convergence and arbitrage.

### Arbitrage
The simultaneous purchase and sale of an asset in different markets to profit from a price discrepancy. Arbitrage activity narrows price differences.

### Incentives Shape Behavior
People respond predictably to incentives. For example, compensating victims of natural disasters might encourage people to settle in high-risk areas. Similarly, policies aimed at reducing obesity may have limited impact if they don't address the underlying incentives that lead to unhealthy choices. Consider how your own actions and decisions are influenced by market signals and government policies.
