## Executive Summary
This introductory guide outlines core grain marketing principles for western Canadian prairie farmers, emphasizing the development of a structured marketing plan, evaluation of pricing and contracting options, understanding basis and hedging, leveraging storage strategies, and implementing disciplined decision-making to manage risk and enhance profitability. This packet details common grain marketing contracts and risk management tools (deferred delivery, basis contracts, options, pools, etc.), explaining their mechanics, pros, cons, and strategic use for price protection and opportunity capture. This source explains the concept of basis (the difference between futures and local cash prices), how it varies by region and time, and how farmers can use basis signals to make marketing decisions. It also details the costs and risks of using on-farm grain storage as a strategy, including opportunity costs and price protection methods like hedging and deferred delivery contracts.

## Farmer Takeaways
- Create a written marketing plan with six elements: production risk, market analysis, financial position, marketing strategies, actions/timelines, and evaluation.
- Set target prices in the top one-third of the expected annual price range rather than chasing peaks; maintain discipline to sell when targets are hit.
- Forward sell incrementally (e.g., 10-15% at seeding and another 10-15% in late summer) based on yield outlook, avoiding 100% forward sale to protect against crop shortfalls.
- Use grain storage only if the projected price increase net of storage costs is positive; monitor basis and market trends for optimal timing.
- Gather unbiased market information including supply/demand data and stocks-to-use ratios; avoid sources with vested interests.
- Understand contract types (cash, deferred, basis, futures-based) and their risks; consult professionals before committing.
- Review and adjust your marketing plan regularly as market conditions change, using a 12-18 month horizon for pre-seeding, pre-harvest, and post-harvest strategies.
- Contracts let you lock in prices or set price floors before harvest, protecting against market declines.

## Market Heuristics
### Top-Third Pricing Rule
Aim to sell in the top third of the annual price range to consistently beat average prices without attempting perfect market timing.

### Incremental Forward Selling
Forward sell portions of expected production at key stages (seeding, late summer) to lock in prices while preserving flexibility for yield variability.

### Storage Cost Justification
Store grain only when the anticipated price rise exceeds all storage costs; basis strength and market rallies are key indicators.

### Diversify Marketing Tools
Combine cash sales, deferred contracts, and options to spread risk and capitalize on different market phases; avoid over-reliance on one method.

### Deferred Delivery vs. Basis Contracts
Deferred delivery locks in full price (futures + basis). Basis contracts lock in only the basis, leaving futures price open until later. Use a basis contract if you expect a strong futures rally and want to participate, but can handle basis risk.

### Options as aFloor Price Tool
Buying a put option establishes a minimum price (floor) for your grain while letting you sell into any market rally. Cost is the premium; if market doesn't fall, you lose the premium but keep upside.

### Hedging Logic
Hedging means taking an opposite position in futures to your physical grain. If you own grain (long cash), sell futures (short) to lock price. A price drop hurts cash value but gains on futures hedge, offsetting loss.

### When to Use Pool Contracts
Pools are best for smoothing income across a volatile season when you prefer an averaged price over trying to time peaks. Avoid if you need full payment upfront or expect a sharp, sustained rally.

## Risk Watchouts
- Elevator congestion or weather delays can prevent timely delivery, causing missed pricing opportunities.
- Forward selling 100% of expected production risks significant loss if yields fall short and forfeits benefits from post-harvest price rallies.
- Storage expenses (including interest and deterioration) may outweigh price gains if market rallies are weaker than expected.
- Emotional decisions like panic selling during price drops or greed-driven waiting for peaks undermine plan discipline and reduce returns.
- Contract terms vary by company; failure to understand obligations (e.g., delivery dates, quality standards) can lead to penalties or forced sales.
- Basis volatility between local elevators and futures markets directly impacts net price; monitor regional basis patterns closely.
- Sudden shifts in supply (e.g., global crop failures) or demand (e.g., export changes) can rapidly alter price outlooks; stay updated on market fundamentals.
- Contractual obligations: Failing to deliver contracted quantity/quality can result in financial penalty or loss.

- [pages 8-9] Deferred delivery contract: Locks in price (futures minus fixed discount), requires full tonnage/quality delivery, protects against falling markets but misses rallies. Advantage: known return, no margin money. Disadvantage: must deliver full contracted amount.
- [page 9] Futures-based contract: Locks in futures price and delivery period, basis chosen later. Protects against price decline but not limited upside. Disadvantage: Requires guaranteed crop quantity/quality; option premium cost must be overcome by market move.
- [pages 9-10] Basis contract: Locks in basis level, futures price set later (before mid-month of delivery). Advantage: Capture a narrow basis early. Disadvantage: Futures may drop, negating basis advantage; requires delivery in set month; cannot use positive basis in inverted market.
- [pages 10-11] Futures option contract: Right, not obligation, to obtain futures contract. Establishes floor price with upside potential. Producer can offset option for profit; no obligation to any specific grain company. Disadvantage: Premium cost; if exercised, margin calls apply.
- [page 10] Production contract: Pre-seeding agreement on variety, acreage, tonnage, and price (often not futures-linked). Guarantees market for part of crop. Disadvantage: Fixed price portion may be below post-harvest market; clarify 'act of God' clause for crop failure.
- [pages 10-11] Flat price delivery agreement: Grain company offers fixed, attractive price for limited time on specific tonnage, often on a narrow basis. Simple and low-risk. Disadvantage: Opportunity is fleeting; prices may rise after you commit.
- [page 11] Grain pricing delivery agreement (target price): Producer sets target price; sale triggers automatically when market hits it. Can cancel any time before execution without penalty. Disadvantage: May price out too low in rapid rally; should be combined with other protection.
