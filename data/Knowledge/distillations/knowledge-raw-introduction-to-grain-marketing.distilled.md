## Executive Summary
Developing a disciplined grain marketing plan is essential for western Canadian farmers to manage price and production risks. This guide outlines foundational strategies, including calculating break-even costs, setting firm target prices, and using incremental forward pricing rather than selling all at once. It details the operational mechanics of contracting tools like deferred delivery, basis contracts, and options to balance pricing flexibility with delivery obligations and margin requirements. Additionally, it highlights how to interpret local basis levels as signals for immediate sale versus storage, while emphasizing the critical need to calculate all physical and opportunity costs of on-farm storage. Ultimately, executing sales based on predetermined targets and unbiased market information removes emotion from marketing decisions, protecting farm margins.

## Farmer Takeaways
- Calculate your break-even price by dividing total cost per acre by expected yield to establish a baseline for your marketing plan.
- Set realistic target prices aiming for the top third of the expected annual price range, and sell strictly when targets are hit.
- Forward price incrementally (e.g., 10-15% at seeding, another 10-15% in late summer) rather than all at once to manage production risk.
- Factor in physical storage costs, shrinkage, and opportunity costs like interest on operating loans against projected market rallies before deciding to hold grain.
- Understand contract trade-offs: Deferred delivery secures returns without margin but carries production risk, while basis contracts lock in local premiums but leave futures exposed.
- Options provide pricing flexibility without margin call risks, but upfront premiums must be factored into your margin.
- Monitor basis levels across multiple buyers to identify seasonal demand peaks and offload grain when the basis narrows.
- Buying inputs instantly makes you 'long' in the cash market; true hedging requires taking an opposite position on the board to protect that investment.

## Market Heuristics
### Discipline Over Emotion
Execute sales immediately when pre-set target prices are hit. Avoid holding out for the absolute top in a rising market or panic selling at the bottom of a falling one.

### Incremental Forward Selling
Capture strong pricing opportunities during spring or summer rallies by forward selling portions of the crop as yield certainty increases throughout the season.

### Storage Must Pay
Only store grain when the forecasted increase in commodity prices is sufficient to cover the financial, physical, and opportunity costs of storage.

### The Premium Hurdle
When using options, any favorable market move must exceed the cost of the premium before the strategy actually pays off compared to a straight cash sale.

### Flat Price Urgency
When a grain company flashes an aggressive flat price, it usually means they are short on immediate logistics; act quickly because tonnage limits fill fast.

### Narrow or Positive Basis
Signals that local demand is outpacing supply. Buyers are aggressively bidding to attract deliveries, making it the ideal time to sell and minimize marketing costs.

### Wide Basis
Signals abundant local supplies and low buyer competition, typical during harvest. Usually a cue to store grain and wait for the basis to narrow.

## Risk Watchouts
- Forward selling 100% of expected production before harvest exposes the farm to massive financial risk if crop yields fail, as you must cover the shortfall.
- Waiting until harvest to consider pricing often leads to missed spring/summer rallies and exposes you to harvest-pressure price drops.
- Logistical bottlenecks like congested elevators or bad weather can prevent delivery and cause you to miss spot pricing opportunities.
- Locking in a basis contract in a falling futures market negates the benefit of the narrow basis unless you are otherwise hedged.
- Assuming a production contract protects you from crop failure is dangerous; actively confirm the inclusion of an 'act of God' clause.
- Exercising an option to take the underlying futures contract instantly exposes you to margin call risks.
- Storing unpriced grain is pure speculation; falling markets will drain net returns while physical storage and interest costs continue to rise.
